Coffee Breakdown

For my Typography and Interaction class, we were asked to design and build a single-serving site (SSS); a website composed of a single page with a dedicated domain name and which serves only one purpose.

After exploring a few different concepts in Sketch, I settled on the domain name coffeebreakdown.com. The idea behind my site is quite simple; it‚Äôs a website which serves to break down the ingredients found in coffee drinks.


Concept

Terms like Affogato, Breve and Ristretto can be quite confusing to a non coffee connoisseur, so I wanted to design a site which you could check whilst standing in line at a coffee shop, wondering what to order.

I was inspired by coffee infographics found on sites such as Pinterest and Tumblr. I added elements of interactivity by providing descriptions of the coffee types on hover, and CSS animations of the ingredients on click.


Future Iterations

My next steps are to add media queries for different breakpoints in order to optimise the site for different devices, and to possibly include translations of what the Italian/Spanish/Portuguese names mean. Click here to view a prototype of the site so far.

Responsive Contexts

Another project brief from my Typography and Interaction class was to design a digital interface for a poem which works across three specific devices and responds to the affordances inherent in each.

We were asked to consider the poem‚Äôs context, as well as how the design might change to accommodate the three platforms we'd chosen. In what ways is it the same? In what ways is it different?


Poem

The poem I picked was ‚ÄúResum√©‚Äù by Dorothy Parker. It‚Äôs quite a dark poem but it also has a humorous and sarcastic undertone, and there is a ray of hope at the end. It reads as follows:

Razors pain you;

Rivers are damp;

Acids stain you;

And drugs cause cramp.

Guns aren‚Äôt lawful;

Nooses give;

Gas smells awful;

You might as well live.

The contexts I decided to design for were standard desktop, mobile and Apple watch. I had several initial ideas ranging from a simple scrolling page with text weaving in and out of fixed SVG elements, to a neon interface which displayed different lines of the poem on hover/click.


Desktop

The route I developed has a different interaction for each of the three viewports. On desktop, the viewport is divided into a 4x2 grid with each section representing a line from the poem. CSS animated vectors appear when you hover over each section. When you click the section is removed; revealing the entire poem line by line.


Phone

On mobile, the poem is read in a continuous scroll with the vectors providing a background to each line of text. As you scroll through each section the vectors collapse on top of each other, resulting in a mish-mash of random shapes at the end.


Watch

On the Apple watch, the poem is read by swiping right-to-left on each line. The vectors once again provide backgrounds to the text, and as you swipe through the poem each section is introduced as a new layer on top of the previous.


Future Iterations

So far I have optimised the site for desktop, which you can view by clicking here. My next steps are to write media queries for the mobile and watch breakpoints, and to test out how the site actually functions on these devices.

I also need to fix a bug which is causing the noose vector to appear twice on hover. There is a harsh cut-off point on the shadow of each vector which is only apparent once sections have been removed, so this is another area that I need to take a second look at.
